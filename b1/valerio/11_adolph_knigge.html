Grammar Rules and Observations

This text contains several important grammatical structures common in German, particularly the imperative mood for giving advice, subordinate clauses, and the use of modal verbs.

1. The Imperative Mood (Imperativ)

The last paragraph is a perfect example of the imperative, used for commands, advice, or instructions. It's directed at "du" (you, informal). The verb is used in its stem form, often without the "-st" ending, and placed at the beginning of the sentence. For separable-prefix verbs, the prefix goes to the end.

Examples from the text:

Gehe nie von deinen Grundsätzen ab! (Never depart from your principles! - from "abgehen")

Handle immer konsequent! (Act always consistently!)

Sei, was du bist... (Be what you are... - irregular form of "sein")

Respektiere dich selbst... (Respect yourself...)

2. Subordinate Clauses with Modal Verbs

The text frequently uses subordinate clauses (Nebensätze) introduced by conjunctions like welches, wohin, dass, and wenn. When a modal verb (like `muss`, `darf`, `kann`, `soll`) is present in such a clause, the conjugated modal verb goes to the very end, after the main verb's infinitive.

Examples from the text:

...welches Besteck man ... verwenden muss. (...which cutlery one must use.)

...wohin man seine Serviette legen darf. (...where one may place one's napkin.)

...dass ein ehrlicher Mann ... nicht alles machen kann und will. (...that an honest man cannot and will not do everything.)

3. Genitive Case (Genitiv)

The genitive case expresses possession or belonging ("of the..." or "whose"). The text shows two forms: one with a noun phrase and one with a relative pronoun.
